History and Description

Sprint races are the oldest recorded form of athletic competition, dating at least to the Olympic Games of 776 B.C. Some sources indicate that the stade race (approximately 200 meters) was the only event at the first Olympic Games. Eventually, a two-stade race was added. Hurdles, like many track and field events, were popularized in British competitions in the 19th Century.

The first modern Olympics (for men only) in 1896 included 100- and 400-meter races and a 110-meter hurdles event. The 200-meter dash and 400-meter hurdles were added in 1900, with the 4 x 100 and 4 x 400 relays entering competition in 1912.

Women's events were added in 1928, including the 100-meter run and the 4 x 100 relay. Further additions included the 80-meter hurdles in 1932 (replaced by the 100 hurdles in 1972), the 200 in 1948, the 400 in 1964, the 4 x 400 relay in 1972 and the 400 hurdles in 1984.

Hurdling can be defined as the act of running and jumping over an obstacle at speed. In the sport of athletics, hurdling forms the basis of a number track and field events which are a highly specialized form of obstacle racing. In these events, a series of barriers known as hurdles are set at precisely measured heights and distances which each athlete must pass by running over. Failure to pass over, i.e. by passing under, or intentionally knocking over hurdles will result in disqualification. Accidental knocking over of hurdles is not cause for disqualification, as the hurdles are weighted to make this disadvantageous. (This is the most commonly misunderstood aspect of hurdling. Through my years in high school, I had many people ask if this was illegal, but it is not illegal, but also not recommended.)

Personal Bio

To introduce myself, I am a former high school hurdler who saw his career end before a collegiate career could blossom. Many injuries (I got pneumonia during my senior track season among other ailments. What 17 year old kid gets pneumonia?) and a decision to focus on academics led me away from track and hurdles, but my heart has remained with them. I still have a wealth of knowledge about hurdling and follow closely along with professional track and field. Coaching high school hurdles would be a great side job for me going forward. Putting all this together, I was thrilled to be able to work on a project that allowed me to learn a little more about hurdling and would hopefully be able to teach other people something about the sport I love.
